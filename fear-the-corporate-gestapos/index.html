Fear the Corporate Gestapos

20 Feb, 2021

Me and my friends often joke that we’re in a bit of nationality, if not identity crisis. Many of us are from Chinese families, born into Thailand, with morality shaped by weekend Japanese cartoons and with our brains quite Hollywoodized. Sometimes we can’t even tell what motives are driving us to believe and behave in a certain way. It’s kinda a conversation guaranteed to come up a booze and weed smoking party.

And for the record, I don’t do weeds or alcohols. The only weeds I consume are different kinds of green tea. Somehow it became a joke that what you order in a bar determined how high of morality you had. My memorized answer had always been “Milk”, and with that you can judge how creative your friends are based on their response. On one end of the spectrum, you have those with a twisted facial expression – “You… seriously? For someone like you? I don’t believe it.”, “What are you, a baptized monk?”. One the other end, it can get very very nasty - “Sure, do you want it in a glass or in a human organ?” or “Do you still want a straw?”.

Apart from Morality, the other topic guaranteed to come up is Freedom and Oppression. I still remember the conversation about how our parents had been brought up, the hardships they had faced and why they had really really wanted us to learn Chinese. They didn’t tell us much the details how all the Chinese schools were completely eradicated from the society. It was not that they saw China would rise to power like today and suddenly their kids could do better, after all this was pretty much around the second half of the 80s. At that time, my grandma was still sending money back to her hometown in Jie-Yang. Lives in Thailand were considered much more well to do than where she came from. If you need to reuse all kinds of excretion for growing food, you know that you’re living in some kind of a hell.

Looking back in hindsight, making sure that kids know Chinese was more about the determination to not let their traditions and beliefs disintegrate into oblivion, plus as a way to get back at the society who used to ridiculed them. It was more to teach you must know who you are and where you are from than a fad or win big in business. Well, the latter is actually a hidden agenda as Chinese would be more “家己人” towards Chinese. The more they can tell you’re Chinese, the better impression you’ll have on them. Knowing Chinese sets you apart from those with a Chinese appearance. Anyway, it was more as to teaching the pride, principles and cultures than business. Lives are short, but any carvings designed onto people’s heart live long. Time has already proven how strong these ideas come to life and thrive.

“Ahh… you enrolled in a Japanese course? I’m glad you’re learning more stuff but it could have been Chinese…” – Lim Sai Keng, my beloved grandma, the kindest person I will only ever meet again in the afterlife.

Weren’t the Nazis and the Japanese in Nanjing trying to create a better world?

But here is what I think is interesting about fears: These people feared the existence of these “impurities” enough to the point that to them the world was no longer a habitable place if they both were left to coexist. And in order to eradicate the things they feared, they bred and instilled more fears into their people to help them in their eradicative missions. Then people instilled with fears feared the first group enough to use whatever measures and methods to carry out “The Mission”. Then the controlling group also feared that the people seemingly in control may start being rebellious and they could trust no one, so more fears were hence introduced as part of controls. Because germs are our gods

In the old days, when people got sick, they prayed to god. When they recovered, they thanked god. But little did they know that it was actually their microbes, the bacteria and viruses. God is all around us while you can’t see god, so are germs, bacteria and viruses. Maybe they are gods of destruction like COVID19, of creation like Lactobacillus in your cup of yogurt. They can be found in many antidotes and vaccines. In the case of bacteria, some forms of them were your ancestors, perhaps before Adam and Eve. And perhaps, bacteria are your gods all along.3

You get stronger when you live harmoniously with them. Without the right balance, you get sick. Too much bacteria in your stomach, you get diarrhea. Too little of them, you become food intolerant. Everything is about having the right balance may say, but when talking about germs people just want to eliminate them altogether, especially in this pandemic. The idea of using disinfectants to kill 99.9% germs is omnipresent and a must-have on some products, while the opposite is unpleasant if not disgusting. The first time I told my wife about this book by James Hamblin who has stopped showering 6 years ago, she said she wanted a divorce if I’d ever attempt doing such thing.

It’s no point and even futile to completely remove germs in the IT world

The antithesis of Chaos Engineering, unpopular and perhaps a blasphemy to many corporate IT managers, is by injecting faults into a running system will allow you to understand and be proactive about what you can do with the soooo complex system you may have. And the catch is it’s gonna be better if it’s a running production system than your lower environment.

“I love production, YOU should love production. You should go as early and often as possible. Bring the kids. Bring the family. The weather’s amazing. It’s better than Disneyland!” – Josh Long, Spring Developer Advocate

Values with Principles

Leaders set visions, Managers execute. The missing link is always HOWWWWWWWWWWW. You can have another great leader of the century who wants to create a better world and it could result in another Auschwitz or Nanjing (I’m looking at you Mr.Musk after you bought those bitcoins). Here is a twisted syllogism for you living in this twisted world:

Everyone likes pizzas.
There is this pizza topping called Supreme.
Hence, everyone is innately a supremacist.

The answer, I believe, lies in also defining and communicating the principles for the values. As a human, we lose track all the time of WHY we are doing something in the first place. Why did we need a safe environment? Because we’re want our people to risk their lives doing their job for the company. Why did we want to have a secure environment? Because many of you are going to lose your jobs if a breach makes tomorrow headline news. Indeed, these are naive whys, but that’s not the point. The point is by understanding the WHY, you can define HOW much much better like this:

Because we value everyone’s safety both physically and mentally, we prohibit any forms of blames on a person. While we do realize humans can sometimes be a factor leading to an incident, only facts will be gathered mainly to remove the human element.

That may be the worst possible way to paraphrase Etsy’s Blameless Postmortems. Anyone reading some DeafOops stuff could come up with that too, but if you peel the shallow layer out, you can see that there’s both values/goals (Learning from Mistakes) and principles (While also giving people Justice they need) in play here. Another view is they want to work with facts (value/goal/policy), but they want to make sure the facts are not distorted, dramatized, monopolized or withheld due to the possibility of retribution, the only unacceptable failures are failures to speak out (principle). Values without principles lead to Auschwitz – you can become just a bean-counter watching some KPIs go up, but you don’t know if those KPIs might mean more death to the Jews. On the other hand, principles without values make you lost by just going through the motion, and hopefully you don’t end up in a gas chamber.

I didn’t come up this idea myself, but was inspired by Bryan Cantrill’s talk – Principles of Technology Leadership. TBH, I’m still struggling with how values and principles are different because there seems to be a thin line separating the two camps. But I at least wholeheartedly agree with what he said (especially, the famous huslinnnnnn'). You can bring more of the “better angels of our nature” of yourself by having your principles clearly elucidated, and maybe along the way you could be a hypocrite as you can’t hold up to the principles all the time. But hey, aren’t lives worth living this way? At least you can give it a try. Absolutely, not for me, but for your kids, for your family, then for your coworkers and teams. And you can start now by watching Bryan’s talk.

Don’t be the police. Instead, be an artist.

After being with the company for more than a while, I believe that the reason we are where we are has been mainly the police departments of the company. By definition, all police officers are those who learn protect and serve. Protect what? The answer lies in “Law enforcement”. Try to reflect this and see who they are in your company.

My experience led me to view Internal Auditor and IT Security departments of most Fortune 500 companies play this role of “Law Enforcement”. They are inherently incentivized to find faults in how others operate. This is fine and dandy, like your body need you need white blood cells to weed out malignant perpetrators. In the same way, Auditors and IT Security are there to protect the integrity of the operations (“Are you really performing as documented?") or the holistic security of the company’s IT system.

The problem is when they take things too strictly or too literally, and they are equipped with all the power to do so. Very often, I hear horror stories about them and the crux just lies in how these people interpret as transgressions. Because they’re incentivized to find flaws, they’re also apt to interpret the rules so that flaws will emerge. The utmost failure of auditors or IT security team would be to have no comment. Alas, finding something petty or ridiculous is still better than nothing-to-look-here-everything-is-good.

The result, unfortunately, is it becomes the highest priority of the comment receivers as well to address these “gaps”. The good, valid ones are ok. But what about those petty and ridiculous ones? What I have seen is they add more toils on top of how the team will have to operate. They twist and turn the limbs until the team becomes an ugly bonsai tree.

What’s an alternative? Personally I think the answer lies in the area almost the opposite of a corporate settings or Science and Technology. It’s art.

In The Phoenix Project, the character that people resonate best is John Pesche, who later transforms himself from a principal-like security officer to a person willing to help others and even takes certain risks. To me, it’s like he is reborn overnight, instead of instituting fears and being a showstopper, becoming a person using his knowledge to say yes because he knows the risks well. Isn’t that like knowing the rules so he breaks them like an artist? And to me, this is the mindset that would make DevSecOps work. The same goes with Auditors. That mindset would make the work more interesting instead of dreadful. Who knows you may have a lot of fun being a part of something bigger and better than a pesticide. Like if you can produce music with good rhythm, I’m sure together we will mix well:

Diversity?? It begins with a two-way communication!

Not sure if you have heard these things before:

I asked you to do so. If not, I will have to go to your VP to get you do it.

It’ll become a mandate for these security tools to be in your pipeline. They are not doing anything much but we want them to be installed.

It must be done, period.

The moment you hear these words, you can probably deduce that Wow these guys will not listen, neither will my manager. That has been my ultimate theory in the corporate settings about power struggling and these people must have accumulated quite amount of power and they’re turned loose to use it. I can’t help but to think how similar this is to the famous Stanford Prison Experiment where things turned awry and had to be stopped even before midway of the experiment.

One thing can be concluded for sure is these people won’t listen. They are not there to hear your explanation. And the last thing they want is to see you challenge their assumptions. It doesn’t matter how good your explanation is or how flawed their arguments are, once pinpointed and fixated, they have the power to declare certain things in a certain way. One of the famous quotes of my ex-VP was “Trust, but verify” and boy oh boy these people come to verify hard with no question can be asked because of the fears being instituted in the past.

Apart from this making me feel angry, if we carefully examining what is not right about the situation, it’d be that the power is unbalanced where one side’s arguments are heavier than the other side. Like how a country falls and when a civil war erupts, it’s mostly because power is not distributed well, or when justice is not properly given. It’s not about politics, but well more of how people live together, cooperate, collaborate, become united or drifted apart.

I’m not in any management position, nor am I a leader, but if I could suggest, it’d be that people will have to be tolerant of different opinions and that should be a foundational habit of an organization. With everything going on, I just see many try to show how diverse their environments are. It’s mostly like Look at us! We have people of colors, we have Asians, we have people of alternative sexual identities. Hence we have diversity! Wrong! That’s merely a theater and make people turn sour once they know their opinions are not welcome. It’s about tolerance in all senses of one’s body, not different sets of RGB value or genomes.

You can look at this how your relationship is with your children. I don’t know about you but Asian parents don’t like their children to challenge them. The downside is that if you treat your people like children, your people behave like children. From a security perspective, to survive in a harsher and harsher cyberspace, don’t you want people (or children?) to be strong? Together, we can be better but we must be able to respect each other’s opinions and learn that we can be wrong sometimes because:

The only real progress lies in learning to be wrong all alone. – Albert Camus

So two-way communication and acceptance are a must. And for egality’s sake, both sides must be able to trust but verify as well.

Say no to Hero culture

I used to be a kid, naive and immature. I used to believe in Heroes because… why not, right? They are good people, they protect us. They fight the bad guys for us.

The danger of this kind of thinking of mine lies into these simple words of the previous sentence: good and bad. Just who defines them in the first place? Is everything the heroes do are rightfully so? It’s good if we have the same sense of good and bad. The problem is that we probably sometimes don’t and when that happens, how the conflicts are resolved.

Corporations are full of heroes. These heroes earned their titles because of leadership, skills, nobility and maybe perception of the crowd the in past. It’s easy to have affection and even fall in love with them, because they’re somebody you can look up to. That’s probably a Halo effect in action, and because of that, it’s hard to challenge them.

Another keyword is also “in the past”, which may or may not have anything to do with now. People, especially at the higher up positions, have little time, hence need to delegate certain decisions to certain people they trust. It’s easier to trust people with a brand than nobody, but is that the right thing to do? The difference of the words already make it clear - easier is not necessarily better. In practice, when you leave certain decision rights to a certain person, a hero, you allow the person to define a certain thing or even become it. For a hero, it may be justice or they become justice. For an IT Security expert, it may be what constitutes security or he can become security. For a centralized Agile and DevOps organization, they get to say what DevOps is and can even monopolize it.

Heroes or experts naturally don’t want you to challenge them either (What do you know about these things? I know it all!). If they think your team are cowboy coders, you’re doomed either directly by them or indirectly by their comments to your management. This can become a vicious cycle and turn an environment more toxic. Those who stay would eye for these heroic positions, where those in the positions would do anything to protect their power and status quo. Those who are tired would just leave. Well, power tends to corrupt, eh?

This is sometimes really hard to see because you’re supposed to love these heroes. But luckily, the trends these days, especially in the DevOps area are promoting the opposite - selflessness over heroic individuals. Some are even denouncing or urging people in the industry to fix a hero culture.

I have a litmus test for this hero culture using a bit of data and statistics (isn’t the trend to be cool these days to say I’m data driven :P). If 9 out of 10, you trust a particular person on a particular topic to make a decision, something is off. Worse, if that person is even from a different organization, congratulations you have a hero! Hopefully, you don’t have too many before they think you’re Thanos and it’ll become another End Game.

Parting words

As an individual, we want no trouble. That’s understandable. Likewise, When we see people on the street start fighting, we don’t want to get in between them as you might become the target instead. When we see an agent of fear operate, we look down and mind our own business.

At the same time, as humans, we have the ability to think critically. We also have the ability, the freedom to speak out. One may argue that maybe it’s a luxury think, a privilege to be able to do so. I don’t think so. As a whole, if we aim for the better, for us, for the people around us, for the place we belong to, I think it’s the moral goal that we’re obliged to pursue. I believe that’s something the founders carefully thought of and believed in the best of the people when they wrote their principles and perhaps even the first set of corporate policies. Sadly along the way, it’s also people who lost sight and may have turned things sideways. The moment those who care discovered it, it may have already been Auschwitz or Nanjing and they may no longer have the power to correct things.

The future lies in how much people can unite and oppose certain things such as the use of fears or authoritarians. It can be hard, because you and your team may already be divided and conquered, or even about to be. You can start now by thinking deeply about things around you, by not simply giving in or by declining your Corporate Gestapos to achieve their eradicative mission. That’s a noble thing one can do.
