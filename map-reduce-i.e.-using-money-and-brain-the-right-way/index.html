Map Reduce is the quintessential of problem solving using both brains and money. While reading it, you can’t help but be impressed how they elegantly tacked the problem and this was in 2004 when computing resources were so scarce. Perhaps some of your phones these days are more powerful than some of their servers. However, they were able to process 1TB of data within easily within 1000 seconds, something many are still struggling with today.

The secret sauce? Simple, using brains to create value from something cheap. It’s almost always the theme of these papers. For Map Reduce, it’s creating a fleet of cheap, commodity machines that would coordinate with each other to process a gigantic amount of data (i.e. all the web pages) if the task can be sequenced into the map and reduce steps. If anything fails, something will pick it up. No manual monitoring, no human intervention, no nonsense that will be required to squeeze more efficiency by outsourcing it some other countries later. Yeah they’ve got money, but they will buy their servers cheap. They even got to the point that they bought grade B memory (need to find source, but I could remember it was an ACM talk) and started in-memory computing before anyone. They rather buy to acquire more power than just to solve a problem. And that’s been making them stay above others, by using brains and not having to do much with being a tech company.

Those who have done functional programming would be very well familiar with the Map Reduce model. I suspect that the popularity of Map Reduce may be the reason why most languages now support functional paradigm. For those who are not, a Map step does what its name suggests - mapping from something to another. That something is usually a key value pair (e.g. a website url as the key and its content as the value). A map step can be used for filtering as well. And you can have a sequence of multiple map steps, like multiple Mario pipes attached to each other. A reduce step does the final calculation from the input (e.g. group and count the occurrence of a word).

The beauty of this model is that both Map and Reduce steps can be distributed to many workers. They can be repeated as many times as you want as long as you keep them deterministic. Remember that deterministic function you learned in highschool? They are the same thing - the output of a function is determined solely by the input. For those of you from the House of API Haute Couture, it’s kinda like but not the really same as your idempotency The premise is that if you call them with the same inputs, they will produce the same results.

So what can you with this? Because their results will be same, you can basically run them anywhere, and you can run them as many as you want as long as you have a machine for them. And because of that, you can buy a lot of cheap machines and distribute them to those machine. And if any of them fails, the rest can pick up easily, you just need to coordinate them. And no, you don’t need human to constantly monitor and coordinate (*cough *cough like a …. Bat&& team) to later outsource them to gain efficiency, you automated them. They built something to automatically coordinate the work and reassign tasks in case of failures. That something piece is called the Master.

Map Reduce seems cool.. So what?

The paper already summarizes pretty much all the benefits, but here I will highlight the points I particularly like.

First, there are certain differences between a pet project and a systematic approach in trying to solve a problem. The first attempts to achieve the state of being cool, while the latter details as much as possible to problem being solved. Throughout the Map Reduce paper, you can clearly see the detailed limitations and resources they were equipped with. I couldn’t help but think of what Dave Thomas and Andy Hunt mention in their book:

“It’s not whether you think inside the box or outside the box. The problem lies in finding the box - identifying real constraints.” – Dave Thomas and Andy Hunt, The Pragmatic Programmer

Secondly, the proposed programming model is a prime example of great separation of concerns in practice. Map Reduce programmers can focus on their map and reduce functions, keep them simple and ensure they are correct. They don’t have to worry about how to make them run in parallel as the framework will handle it for them. The operators of the cluster can just focus on resource allocation and observe how the system performs.

Lastly, and I love this one most, how it uses redundancy to deal with stragglers. A straggler is a machine or process that takes an unsually long time to complete one of the last few tasks in the computation. There can be so many reasons why stragglers occur, but instead of focusing on finding the rootcause for each of them, the Master assigns a backup job to other worker(s). If the backup finishes first, it doesn’t have to care about the straggler anymore. Redundancy may sound like a taboo for some people, but they were able to achieve 44% increase in terms of performance. It’s like if you’re slow, I’m gonna send in Usain Bolt.

Think about it, the same approach can be used for not just computing, but how your organization can achieve its goals as well. Gregor Hohpe refers to this as The Economy of Speed. In that type of economy, there’s no reason why there must be just one group responsible for a task. If they’re in your way, you can find a Usain Bolt to get the value to the customer without waiting for the authorative group to come back to you. IIRC, Mike Amundsen said in one of the podcast episode that Amazon has many of the same things because of no wait policy as well.

Epilogue

I hope by now I was able to convince you that problem solving has something to do with using brains and money the right way than just being recognized by other as a tech company. If you don’t, then please at least don’t discourage others who aspire to. Sometimes these tech companies you don’t want to become resort to using a number of primitive approaches than you might think (like pingging and piggy backing).

Finally, Map Reduce is how Google solved this data processing problem back in 2004. In 2014, they announced they no longer use the technique. The paper did become a classic on how to use brains and money the right way. And the news basically gave us a good reminder that if you’re still struggling with the same problem they had in the past, the future doesn’t really bode well for you (plus, they have a much better way of indexing your lives too).
